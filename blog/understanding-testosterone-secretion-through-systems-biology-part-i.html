Understanding Testosterone Secretion Through Systems Biology: Part I

Date Tue 03 December 2019 Tags systemsbiology / testosterone / modelling

In this series of blog posts, I would like to show how some of the physiological processes in our body can be understood from the systems biology standpoint and how we can analyze these processes using the mathematical methods and computational modelling. Specifically, we will try to model and analyze the regulation of the male sex hormone Testosterone by Luteinizing Hormone and Luteinizing Hormone-Releasing Hormone. We will look at:

  • Dynamics of the hormonal secretion in the normal physiological conditions
  • Effects of the castration and constant external testosterone dosing on the hormone concentrations
  • The onset of the andropause and its consequences for the hormonal levels
  • The role of the external dosing of hormones on the mitigation of the andropause-related changes

However, in the first part of of the series, I will try to provide some (non-expert!) background information about testosterone and associated hormones and explain why this problem is actually important. Also, I will try to outline the process of qualitative reasoning about the feedback systems in our body.

The computational analysis that I will do later on will be based on the old, but a still important paper by William R. Smith, which comes from the 1980s [2].

So let's start with hormones!

Hormones¶

Hormones are powerful chemical messengers that our bodies secrete to achieve various physiological responses and to control the state of the organism. There is a wide range of hormones that our bodies manufacture: for example, the role of hormone insulin is to allow cells to store the energy (for instance, when you eat that delicious blueberry muffin), the role of adrenaline is to increase the heart rate and provide you with an immediate bout of energy (for instance, if you are chased by a lion and your life actually depends on how fast you can run).

Another important class of hormones are the sex hormones. In males, the dominant sex hormone is testosterone, which is released mainly from the testes (a small fraction is actually released from the adrenal glands) and which is responsible for the development of the secondary sexual characteristics, like muscle growth, deep voice, and hairy forearms, and certain behavioural traits, like susceptibility to risk-taking and increased... assertiveness - if you have seen any of the Die Hard or Rambo movies, you know what I am talking about.

Andropause and TRT¶

We know that the levels of sex hormones start increasing in the puberty (roughly at the age of 11) and start decreasing in the middle-aged men (after 40). A decrease in the levels of sex hormones is associated with the low libido, decreased energy levels, and loss of the muscle mass. This phenomenon has been called andropause and is, in principle, similar to the menopause that women experience.

The decrease in the levels of testosterone is definitely not a pleasant experience. While there are some lifestyle interventions that can help increase the natural testosterone levels, many middle-aged men resort to the Testosterone Replacement Therapy (TRT), when the Testosterone is supplemented externally through various methods, including skin creams, inhalers, and intramuscular injections (ouch!). The external testosterone is also often (mis)used by the bodybuilders because of its role in increasing muscle mass and strength

However, such external testosterone therapy is not without potential side effects, including acne, swelling, breast enlargement, liver toxicity, and heart disease [4]. Consequently, it is important to understand the effects of the TRT on the human body and to analyze its benefits. As an engineer, I would immediately ask the question: "Could I apply the mathematical modelling and computational methods to determine the external dose of the hormones needed to achieve the physiological levels as needed?" I think that answering the questions like this is the best way for bioengineers to improve the human conditions.

Biological background and negative feedback¶

The first thing that we need to do is to understand the basic biology behind the processes we would like to model. We have to select the reasonable depth of the biological description, which requires careful thinking. The selection of biological model that is too simple, will prevent us from capturing of the biological behavior that we are interested in, while choosing the biological description that is too complex will result into the model that is not tractable (we cannot use the mathematical tools to analyze it) or is computationally too expensive (imagine we want to simulate every single molecule of Testosterone in the body - that would require a lot of computational time, and time is money).

So how does the Testosterone secretion work from the (somewhat simplified) biological point of view?

In humans, the Luteinizing Hormone-Releasing Hormone (LHRH), which is produced in the hypothalamus in the brain, is carried to the pituitary gland in the blood via the hypophyseal portal vessels. In the pituitary gland, LHRH stimulates the secretion of the Luteinizing Hormone (LH), which is then released into the blood and travels to the gonads (testicles, in males), where it stimulates the Testosterone (T) production by the Leydig cells in males, and by the ovaries in females. In females, the majority of Testosterone is then converted to the female sex hormone called estradiol. Subsequently, Testosterone is released into the blood and performs its biological actions, for example, activation of the androgen receptors in the human cells [3], or the actions through the conversion into other molecules like DHT and estradiol.

You could say: "Well, if the LHTH stimulates the production of LH, which stimulates the production of T, how come that these hormonal concentrations do not grow off-charts?"

First, all three hormones also either degrade or dilute in the blood. Second, the release of the LHRH from the hypothalamus seems to be negatively regulated by T. In other words, as the blood concentration of T increases, the secretion rate of the LHRH decreases. This kind of regulatory scheme is absolutely essential in the majority of the biological and man-made systems and is called negative feedback.

Therefore, we can imagine the following scenario:

  1. Let's assume that at some time point, the concentration of T in the blood is low and therefore the rate of inhibition of production of LHRH by Testosterone is low.
  2. This means that initially, the secretion rate of LHRH is higher than its degradation rate, so the concentration of LHRH increases.
  3. The secretion of LHRH stimulates the production of LH, and because secretion rate of LH is increasing with the concentration of LHRH, amount of LH increases as well provided that LH concentration is not too high, causing the degradation/dilution of LH to prevail.
  4. Subsequently, increasing concentration of LH causes the rate of production of T to go up and if the levels of the T are initially low, the degradation/dilution of T is low as well. This means that the concentration of T increases.
  5. However, with the increased blood concentration of T, the inhibition of production of LHRH increases too and the concentration of LHRH drops.
  6. As the concentration of LHRH drops, so does concentration of LH.
  7. When LH levels drop, levels of T drop as well. This means that the strength of the negative regulation of LHRH decreases...
  8. ... And the cycle repeats.

The description above is a bit long, but this kind of thinking process is very important in systems biology - based on the biological principles, we inferred that the qualitative behaviour of the studied system should be oscillatory. And this is actually something that has been observed in the clinical trials and in healthy subjects [5].

So this is the first part of the blog post series about the analysis and modelling of the Testosterone secretion and introduction into the systems biology.

In the next post, we will construct the mathematical model of the Testosterone secretion system and we will try to simulate it computationally. Watch out for the next part!

A practical tip: Appropriate level of Testosterone are important for your health, whether you are woman or man. While preparing this blog post, I found an interesting paper by Luboshitzky et al [6]. This paper investigated the effect of the sleep deprivation on the serum testosterone levels in the young, healthy, adult males. It was found that in the men with the fragmented sleep, there was a significant delay in the testosterone secretion. Furthermore, the nocturnal sleep was only observed in the subjects that entered the rapid eye movement (REM) sleep.

So what is the lesson from above? The high-quality sleep is important for your hormonal health - do not neglect it!
